EulerType

A stroll through the mathematics department of any university would yield walls covered in blackboards and stacks upon stacks of paper bearing a myriad of handwritten symbols.

At a time when the world runs on complex mathematics and extreme computation, our primary technologies for mathematical expression are surprisingly analog. Euler Type, is an attempt at digitizing and augmenting the experience of mathematics.

Context

Typing and reading digital text forms an intrinsic part of our daily experience, yet digital math notation is fairly abscent. While other fields like Computer Science adopted a digital medium for symbolic manipulation, we are still mainly editing math notation using pencil and paper.

The digital tools that we have at our disposal are mainly useful for transcribing and representing symbols, as opposed to letting us work with them.

Math notation as an analog interface

There are numerous publications which document the difficulties intrinsic to handwriting mathematics. They stem primarily from the fact that the practice relies heavily on transcription, symbolic redundancy, and poorly standardized layout schemes.

This entails that as a tool for an exact endeavor, handwritten notation introduces a great margin of error during computation and it imposes an unnecessarily high cognitive load upon the user.

If we model mathematical notation as an analog interface, it becomes evident that static symbols are poorly effective at conveying the context that surrounds an operation. In fact, practitioners tend to obscure certain symbolic steps in an effort to abbreviate the amount of writing

Math notation as a digital interface

Digital interfaces afford more than just symbolic displays. Much like code, the practice of mathematics could be augmented by tools like auto formatting, auto completion, copy-pasting, real-time editing, and debugging.

Symbols themselves could be augmented with useful signifiers like syntax highlighting, tooltips, and other visual strategies that offload memory-intensive tasks from the user.

Mathematics as a fully digital medium

Translating mathematics into a fully digital experience would enable it to become a networked activity, opening up novel avenues for working and communicating mathematics in a global context.

Symbols would also become objects embedded with semantic data that would make it easier to catalogue and search among the corpus of mathematical work.
